I use AI when I write. Not to write for me. To work with me. Here's what that means in practice.

## What AI does

**Structure.** I dump ideas as voice notes or rough bullet points. AI organizes them into a coherent skeleton. It finds the thread I was reaching for but hadn't articulated yet.

**Research.** AI pulls in relevant sources, checks claims, finds examples I might have forgotten. It's faster than my browser history.

**Editing.** First drafts are mine. AI catches repetition, softens clunky transitions, kills filler words. It's a copy editor that works at machine speed.

## What I do

**The thinking.** Every argument, every opinion, every conclusion is mine. AI doesn't decide what to say. It helps me say it better.

**The voice.** AI can mimic my writing style from examples. But it can't have my experiences. The specific stories — the 11 months without Figma, the Age of Empires documentation, the 75,000 downloads — are things I lived. AI synthesizes; humans experience.

**The final pass.** Every piece gets a human review. I read every word. If a sentence doesn't sound like something I'd actually say out loud, it gets rewritten.

## Where the line is

If a piece is mostly AI-generated with light human editing, I'll say so. If it's mostly human-written with AI assistance, I'll say that too.

The default: AI-assisted. That means I wrote the core, AI helped with structure and polish, and I approved every word.

The point isn't to hide the process. It's to be clear about what's mine and what's machine. If that distinction ever gets fuzzy, I'll say so.
