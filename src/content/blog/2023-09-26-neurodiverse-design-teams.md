While organizations have made meaningful strides in gender and racial diversity, neurodiversity remains largely overlooked in team-building and leadership conversations. This is a mistake — and a missed opportunity.

Neurodiverse designers bring distinct strengths: pattern recognition, deep focus, systems thinking, divergent creativity. But traditional management practices — open-plan offices, ambiguous feedback, unstructured meetings — can actively undermine their ability to contribute.

This article is about what inclusive leadership looks like for neurodiverse design teams. Not checkboxes. Not awareness posters. Practical changes to how you structure work, communicate expectations, and design the environment so every designer can do their best work.
